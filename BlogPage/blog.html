Libraries, Knowledge and the universe. An opinion on the works of Vannevar Bush and Jorge Luis Borges

I recently had the pleasure of reading 3 excripts from 2 different novels and 1 essay by 2 excellent writers. The novels were The Library of Babel[1] & The Garden of Forking Paths[2] by Jorge Luis Borges and an essay As We May Think[3]by Vannevar Bush. The stories were ... elaborate, well written and thought provoking. In all the stories I seemed to find a common theme of libraries, collections of information. This theme is most present in The Library of Babel in which Borges talks of the universe which is also known as the library. It is made up of a series of identical hexagonal-shaped rooms. These rooms are filled with books, all the books that one can ever imagine and not imagine. The Library of Babel is filled with all the information in the universe that was ever created and thought of as well as all the information that was never created or though of. It contains all the words ever written and all the words not written… I think you get the point. I found this idea of a library of endless information very similar to the internet. The internet is something we use every single day and its size is continually growing. More users are creating pages and websites, making comments and posting videos. The library of information that is tangible to us on a daily basis is essentially infinite. At the moment the internet is unfortunately finite but its wealth of information is so large that it may seem infinite for a humans comparatively short time on this earth. It’s daunting, Mitch Kapor says it best “Getting information off the internet is like taking a drink from a fire hydrant” [4]. It contains so much information, one could easily compare it to the Library of Babel that Borges describes.

I found all three extracts to touch on the subject of time, and more specifically preserving knowledge and information for the rest of time. The Library of Babel is a collection of information that will exist forever, humans will continuously traverse its depth in search of new information. The Garden of Forking paths continuously presents a strong metaphor for time. “The garden of forking paths” is a metaphor or an idea of the infinitely dividing paths of time. He attempts to convey the message that time is not one singular thing but rather a series of continuously twisting, colliding and diverging paths that all exist in one singular spectrum. Bush touches on the aspect of time and the idea of preserving or extending human knowledge, as well as the ability to collect, arrange and store all of this knowledge for the rest of time. I find what these authors describe to be terrifyingly similar to the internet. The internet is infinite, once something is on the internet it is almost undoubtedly going to remain there for the rest of time or for as long as ‘the internet’ still exists. Just as the library of Babel will exist for ever, and the garden of forking paths will continue to twist through time so will the internet continue to grow, twist and exist.

I for one, stand on the side of the internet and hold it above all other libraries that Borges and Bush may speak off. The reason why I say this, is merely because of the fact that the internet is more than just a library or collection, it is a browser. The internet is seemingly infinite but because it is a browser it can be searched through and ordered. Unlike the Library of Babel one can easily find anything they are looking for on the internet with a simple search on the search bar. No information is hidden, nothing is unobtainable. All the information of the world is at our finger tips with one click of a button. The internet is an extraordinary invention, it is the triumph of humanity.

So what exactly is a browser? In today's society, the main browsers we come across are web browsers like google which I spoke about earlier. Browsers are generally collections of information, like libraries, but we can search through and order all of it. A web browser or internet browser is a software application that lets people from all over the world access the world wide web [1]. Basically, it gives you access to the entirety of the world's information. Google Chrome, Safari, Mozilla Firefox, Opera, and Microsoft Edge are all browsers that the world commonly uses on a daily basis.

Web browsers generally work by sending and receiving information to and from other parts of the web. When the information is received it is displayed on your browser window. Text, images, and video files are transported around the web using Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP)[2]. Web browsers use a user interface to display information graphically, this is known as the frontend and it allows for navigation of web pages. However, data is handled in the backend. The backend (such as servers and databases) sends data out across the internet, based on your actions, and carries the data received back to you so that you can connect to the websites you visit and interact with them.

The medium is the message, an interesting idea about the way we view things in society

I recently had the pleasure of listening too and reading some of Marshall McLuhan, a canadian philosopher’s, work. He speaks extensively of the idea that “the medium is the message” [1]. He attempts to convey the idea that nothing is in itself inherently good or bad. Everything in the world exists neutrally. It is how this thing is used that determines its constitution. For instance “Firearms are in themselves neither good nor bad; it is the way they are used that determines their value.” I believe this to be completely true, and something that is seen very prevently in today's society. I feel the best example one can use to explain this, is social media and the internet as a whole. The internet exists as a completely neutral platform. It is neither good nor bad, it is only a library. A place for us to store thoughts, ideas and creations. How we intend to use these thoughts, ideas and creations determines whether it is something positive or negative, good or bad. For instance advertising. Advertising is at the forefront of all development. Advertising can be seen anywhere, on the internet, the television or even on the road while you drive. Advertising is not good or bad, and it generally has no effect on us. However, what is being advertised does have an effect on us. An advert telling you to kill puppies would be considered inherently bad would it not? What if the advert told you to kill puppies for the sake of mankind's survival? Would it then still be considered ‘bad’.

The point I am trying to highlight is that not all information is inherently good or bad, almost all of the things we see and hear today have hidden agendas behind them. It is impossible to place anything we see these days on a side between good or bad as the lines of good and bad have been mixed together and blurred. Nowadays it is up to us to decide the nature of things. And this is something that is becoming increasingly more difficult.

Humans have different views on different things, it is our differences that make us unique is it not? So with this said I believe that is extremely important that we stay vigilant in our daily lives and put more thought into the things we read online. With fake news being so prevalent it can be very tricky to tell whether the information you are receiving is legitimate. Marshall details the dangers of this situation well. “The paradox of mechanization is that although it is itself the cause of maximal growth and change, the principle of mechanization excludes the very possibility of growth or the understanding of change.” Meaning that although we have the means to create growth and change through the gain of new information, more often than not we are unable to predict what growth that change or new information will cause. And whether that growth will be beneficial or detrimental to society. I believe that as the world becomes more educated we are better able to use the information we receive for good. As global communication improves it becomes harder to keep things hidden. Agendas are displayed openly on News headlines, people's lives and thoughts are contained in websites and social media posts and captions. We are all becoming more and more connected and as we grow and connect it is up to us to prosper this growth in a positive direction. One that will benefit all members of society. Growth is neither inherently good or bad, it is why we grow, what we grow into and what that growth is used for that determines its value.

Innovation through minimal effort, a brief history of web robots

We all know what robots are. We have seen them in movies like Terminator and i-robot, or those metal arms that are in factory production lines and NASA science videos. However, did you know that there are also robots that exist on the internet or on our computers and phones? Web robots or ‘bots’ are in fact extremely common and we use or get affected by bots on a daily basis. For instance, Googlebot [1], which allows us to quickly find the most relevant search results on Google by searching through millions of webpages in seconds. Websites use chatbots to communicate with their visitors. There are also malicious bots such as GTbot [2] which was one of the first malicious botnet programs created in 2000 that was capable of denial of service attacks (DoS attacks [3]). These attacks are cyber-attacks which are designed to shut down a machine or network and make it inaccessible to the users trying to access it. Another botnet program that emerged in 2007, Storm [4], was believed to have infected over 50 million computers and was used for many types of crimes including stock price fraud and identity theft.

Bots are woven into today’s society and carry out almost all menial tasks that occur on the internet. It is common practice for computer programmers to make bots that perform functions for them, in fact it is one of the first things you will be taught to do if you learn to code in a computer language. Websites, programs, applications and even video games use bots for all sorts of purposes. I personally think it is quite brilliant thinking. Imagine you are a video game designer and you have to create a video game. You are going to have millions of lines of code to create, sift through and test. Quite honestly it seems like an impossible task, but because of bots and really clever people, we have found ways to make this task possible. For instance testing bots, if you’ve got a million lines of code and a couple thousand functions running at once you will want ways of ensuring that all the functions are running correctly before you even start the program, otherwise things are going to mess up big time somewhere along the way. So people create testing bots that are used to test functions before they run to see if they are working correctly. These things become quite important when you are working with people’s lives, for instance NASA engineers who design rockets, their programs need to have the speed and accuracy that bots provide in order to tell them if something is going wrong before it becomes a problem. Or if you work in artificial intelligence, when creating neural network programs that can sometimes take months on end to run you want to make sure everything is running correctly before you start it.

So, why am I telling you all this information about bots? Well it’s because I think they are really important, we’ve been using them all this time, so we should know what they are. They affect us every single day and they can make our lives both easier and more challenging. I believe robots give a very accurate and funny insight into human nature. Bots are a perfect example of human intelligence coupled with human laziness. We’re so smart that we create things that do things for us, so we don’t have to do them again.

Quite funny, isn’t it.

Usability & Accessibility on the web

Today I was presented with an interesting idea about Usability and accessibility on the internet. When I heard those words, at first I had no idea what they actually meant. But after looking it up I found out that web accessibility is a seriously important thing and so is web usability. Web accessibility [1]allows for people with disabilities to equally perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with websites and tools and it allows them to contribute to the internet freely. Usability refers to the effectiveness of the design of a product or in this case website.

Both these ideas are two significantly important factors that one has to take into account when creating things on the web. I for one had not thought of this until reading about it and feel rather ignorant for not doing so. And the thing is, a lot of people don’t think about it. Disabled people make up 15% of our population, that’s a significant amount of people, around 1 billion to be specific. So how can we improve our designs to cater to the needs of these people?

Some ways [2] might be in how you make the website look, using high contrast can benefit people on mobile devices, especially in sunlight or a dark room. Captions and text allow people in noisy or quiet environments as well as people who are hearing impaired to utilize videos or sounds. Websites need to ensure that assistive reading technology such as screen readers and screen magnifiers that enlarge content works on the website. Voice recognition software used to input text, as well as text to speech technology, are both important and must be thought about. It is important to take these factors into account so that people with disabilities aren’t excluded from using the web.

A brilliant definition of usability by ISO 9241-11 [3] is “The extent to which a product can be used by specified users to achieve specified goals effectively, efficiently and with satisfaction in a specified context of use.” It is important that websites are designed with these goals and contexts in mind so that no barriers are created that exclude people from accessing the internet.

An indepth look at “Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information by Mark Graham, Stefano De Sabbata and Matthew A. Zook”

All information in the world has geography. Graham [1]says of information “It is from somewhere; about somewhere; it evolves and is transformed somewhere; it is mediated by networks, infrastructures, and technologies: all of which exist in physical, material places.” This information matters because it shapes our understanding of different places in the world, and how that information is distributed globally. With this information we can determine how information is spread globally, who is utilising this information and how different groups of people try to impose different narratives on informational augmentations. The point Graham intends to prove in his paper is that geographic information is characterised by a host of uneven geographies.

There are approximately 4.72 billion active internet users in the world today [2], and it's growing fast. There are almost 900 000 new users on the internet each day. It is important that we know where these new users are coming from and who exactly is utilising this information. We are able to determine which parts of the world are utilising the most information and how information is being distributed on a global scale i.e. the information that people receive and the geographical location of those people using it. With this data we can more accurately track the flow of data and determine what information different parts of the world are receiving and utilising. This information is important as it can be used to predict growth of different countries or can be used to send more information and resources to less developed countries that might require a greater access to information in order to develop.

Today we have more access to information and data than we have ever had before. Graham points out an interesting example, that at the dawn of the 19th century we had very limited access to information and the tools we had for collecting this data were even poorer. This made large-scale data extremely difficult and relatively rare. There were no resources such as computers and the internet to store data, we only had the pen and page. This caused a lot of restrictions to the collection, spread and preservation of information. However, it also made information far more powerful. As Grahams says “because knowledge and codified information are always produced under conditions of power (Crampton 2008; Pickles 1994), control over hegemonic representations has been a way of exerting economic, social, and political power (Laclau and Mouffe 1985).” This meant that global power houses could control the flow of information. This information divide could clearly be seen when comparing the spread of information in the Northern hemisphere to the spread in the Southern hemisphere. This power over information manifests distinct spatial patterns and almost all mediums of information are characterised by significant spatial inequalities.

Today we have far more tools at hand to collect and track information and information geographies. The invention of the computer and the internet were 2 of the most significant tools created for this purpose. Tools are being developed every day to help improve our data collection and understanding. For instance ‘the semantic web’ [3] which is a movement to make the web more efficient by creating common frameworks that allow data to be easily shared and reused across sites, services and places. OpenStreetMap (OSM) is the world's largest roadmap and it is used as a free, open source base map for thousands of other digital platforms and services. OpenStreetMap gives us some very useful insight and resources as to how information is spread globally. The United States accounts for the largest total amount of content, hosting 21% of all nodes present in OSM. This is followed by France, Canada, Germany and Russia each containing over 100 million nodes. Alone, these 5 countries host almost 58% of all the content in OSM.

Information Geography is extremely prevalent in today's society. Information has become one of the most powerful resources on this planet, wars are fought using information, we bargain with information. Information gives power. That's why it is important that we highlight areas of information connectivity and disconnectivity as understanding geographies of access and enablement provides important insights into the distribution of technologies and services that are essential for digital communication, participation and representation. In doing so we can utilise this information to highlight areas in the world that need improvement or are lacking in one way or another. By doing so we can use our understanding of the spread of information and information geographies to more accurately improve the spread and flow of information so we can assess the requirements of each place in the world and more accurately cater to their informational needs. It is important that we use the tools we have at hand to continuously strive for the betterment of all human life, we must use these tools to grow together.

What is information geography and why does it matter

Information geography refers to the way information is created, used, and distributed throughout the world. Specifically, it refers to where Internet content is being created, whether the amount of content that gets created in different places changes over time and the ways in which landscapes of content have been structured and formed (Mark Graham 2014) [1]. It is important that we consider digital divides in the world in order to develop strategies that help us break down those divides so that we can all ultimately benefit from the internet and help include those who are left out of the flow of information.

The internet is not something that exists within a location less cloud, with infinite storage space and bandwidth. The internet is characterised by distinct geographies such as servers and websites. All the tiniest bits of information from scripts to search bots must exist somewhere. This is why it is important to understand our geography or data shadows so that we can more accurately include those that are left out of them.

As of January 2021, there were 4.66 billion active internet users [2], which’s almost 60% of our population. In our past information about the world has been highly geographically contained and it took time for news of new discoveries and inventions to travel. We would require media to convey that information or people themselves would have to spread that information, through various methods such as media and word of mouth. Distance has a significant impact on the ability of information to spread. For example, maps from the early 13th and 14th centuries would often contain rather accurate information about the area in which the cartographers would live, however, as you got further and further away from the origin of the cartographer, the information became less accurate and to some degree almost none existent. This is because 13th and 14th-century methods of transportation and communication were far and few and were pretty much limited to ships, words, and books. Today we have significantly more means of transportation and communication that allow for information to spread more easily across the world.

Despite the vast increase in internet connectivity across the world, it can still be seen that there are areas that produce and utilise significantly less information than other areas in the world. A perfect example of this is that there are more than three times as many journals being published in Sweden as there are in the entire continent of Africa [3]. There is a centrality of the world’s information, and it can be found in a few distinct information cores or world powers. It can be seen that there is not a lot of content created by the South and that there is not a lot of content created about the South either. A lot of people, places, and knowledge are being left out because of these divides.

A large part of the solution to this problem of digital divides is to connect the previously disconnected. However, there are many problems such as poverty, famine, education, culture, and religion that play a role in these digital divides. But I believe that it is our goal as a society to strive for a more connected future so that we can tear down these digital divides and grow together as a connected society.

Protecting your privacy on social media

I recently discovered that teenagers are using a network of multiple trusted instagram accounts to post content from different devices in multiple different locations, all in an attempt to stop their data from being tracked . I read this in a CNET article called “Teens have figured out how to mess with Instagram's tracking algorithm”. It makes a lot of sense to me. The world is a scary place and there are very scary people in it. Social media makes us extremely vulnerable, especially when you use it a lot. Everything you do on the internet and through social media gets tracked in some form or the other. Apps like instagram and facebook are able to track what websites you visit, how long you stay on the websites and what you do on them. Advertisements can track what you search for and it really does feel like they are listening to what you say. Ads these days are far too accurate... It's scary.

And this is what gave a highschool student named Samantha Mosley her idea to fool instagrams tracking algorithms. Her and her friends came up with the idea of making multiple instagram accounts and giving access to one of these accounts to someone you trust. You request a password reset and then send the link to that trusted friend who will log on on the new device. Finally get another friend to post the photo from another device. By doing this Instagram will be collecting very random metadata from all these different devices in different locations. If you can do this with say 20 or more devices with 20 different locations, your data will be unreadable. THis is quite an elaborate process to keep your data private but Mosley said "I like knowing that if someone were to find my account, they're not going to be able to track my movement."

As the youth of this generation we were raised on this technology, so it has become deeply integrated into our daily lives. Everyone has facebook and everyone has instagram, but not everyone knows the risks behind it. The simple fact is, if anyone who knows what they are doing with a computer tries hard enough they will be able to access your name, the place you work and probably your physical address too. It makes it very easy to track where a person is going on a day to day basis if you keep a constant live stream of your life going on. It is unfortunate that we have to be concerned with our privacy in this way. As O’Sullivan says "I love that the younger generation is thinking along these lines, but it bothers me when we have to come up with these strategies to avoid being tracked, she shouldn't have to have these psyop [psychological operations] networks with multiple people working to hide her identity from Instagram. The platform should just have an account that works and lets people feel safe about being on social media."

So that's why it's important to know who is looking at your data and to protect your accounts thoroughly. Personally I prefer to keep my data footprint low in order to avoid these unnecessary risks and worries. Thankfully there are tools that are being developed to help protect our privacy, so hopefully one day we will be able to live in a world where we can feel secure on the internet and our privacy can be protected and respected.

Half the tech developers out there release their products into the world with the intent of making it a better place, so why would we make these systems racist? Well thats the point the Dr. Ruha Benjamin [1] is trying to portray in her book Race after technology [2] . Racism is not intentionally designed to be a part of these systems but due to the lack of diversity in system testing and not thinking about these kinds of issues in the design phase, these issues arise.

Dr. Ruha points out a great example of this through automated soap dispensers. A few years ago, there was a video posted to the internet of two friends using an automated soap machine, one friend had a light skin tone and the other a dark. Both friends attempted to use the soap dispenser, however, it would only dispense soap to the lighter toned friend. This example is a perfect idea of how racism can be embedded in technologies. How was it even possible in the first palace that a product with this type of defect could be released for use by the general public. It highlights and provides a lot of detail as to who was behind the scenes in the research and development phases.

Coded inequity or the new Jim code is something that has been prevalent for years but light is only being shone on it now. I believe it is an important issue that needs to be fixed. Race needs to be taken into account when designing systems to cater for the needs of all societal groups. By doing so we can avoid making these ignorant and unintended mistakes of the past. AJames Baldwin says in A Letter to my Nephew “this is the crime of which I accuse my country and my countrymen and for which neither I nor time nor history will ever forgive them, that they have destroyed and are destroying hundreds of thousands of lives and do not know it and do not want to know it.”... “It is the innocence which constitutes the crime.” [3]

Dr. Ruha speaks about the mistake of creating and designing things to fix problems. Ruha believes that it is dangerous to create things to solve solutions as it is difficult to create things in the first place. The systems we originally created are riddled with issues and these issues need to be fixed before we can start a new design. New design creates more problems that need to be solved, this creates a perpetual cycle of creation and solution that never fixes the root issue. So she suggests that as designers we should take part in investing in local efforts that are currently aiming to fix solutions of the past and present. Put our efforts into what is already in place and use those efforts to make what we already have better.

This idea of discriminatory design is very prevalent and as a designer myself I believe it is something I must personally take into account. Dr. Ruha Benjamin speaks strongly about the idea of radicalism, that we must question everything in our day to day lives. Because that is radical, to question constantly in order to make progressive and continuous change in our daily lives for the betterment of all society.

An insight into Jozi’s techno scene

One of the things I fill my time with is techno. Music is one of the things I enjoy the most in this world and Techno is one of my favourite genres. It is an extremely diverse genre of music, with variations in style from hard and fast Berlin acid techno, to soft, slow, and rhythmic dub tech and everything else in between. The focus of this type of music is more on the sound of it than the lyrics, and this is one of the things I enjoy the most about it. The sounds of the songs create emotion within you, unlike most songs that get you to focus on their lyrics in order to draw meaning and emotion from you when listening to it. This is why I feel techno is so well designed for a live experience. With their train-like rhythms, techno sets keep crowds hooked to the dancefloor and make them forget about life as they get lost in the trans-like beats of the song. These moments on the dancefloor, the ones that make you forget about all other moments but the present, are the moments I live for.

South Africa has a very talented but small techno scene, that many would consider ‘underground’. I have been a part of this scene for 4 years now, and it has become one that is very close to my heart. The community is tiny but friendly and loving. I found myself meeting some techno legends almost as soon as I jumped on the scene. Artists like Dogstarr, Avi Subban, The Lazarusman, black coffee, Mark Valsachie, Terry Aronis, G-force, Grant VR, and Fabio are familiar faces that I see almost regularly and have had the pleasure of speaking with many times. This is due to the beauty and interconnectedness of our local music scene. It is composed of a small but dedicated group of people who you will very quickly meet (‘The regulars’ as they’re termed) at almost any event you go to in Jozi.

The techno industry took a big hit over 2020, with the shutdown of clubs due to Covid-19. Many DJs and artists suddenly found themselves with any form of income and no option to make more in the foreseeable future. Even to this day, the techno scene is not what it used to be. Due to the presence of things such as curfew, limited venue capacities and unpredictable alcohol restrictions, SA’s local artists were being faced with constant uncertainty and many struggled to keep afloat. However, thanks to the dedication of SA’s techno lovers and partiers and with the ease of lockdown restrictions and later curfews that we have seen over 2021, the local scene has been able to build itself back up. We went from no events happening whatsoever to multiple events happening Thursday to Sunday every week and if we’re lucky one or two mid-week events.

A lot has happened for techno lovers in SA over the past year. Truth nightclub closed down after 21 years of partying at the start of 2020. MUUDmovement, an event organizer started by my friends Claude, Sheldon, and Anthony almost 4 years ago, has been making ripples in the scene and is slowly starting to take over with their new wave of high-energy acid and hard techno. They’re pushing some really big names behind them and are throwing some gnarly memorable events. AND club has been throwing a slurry of insane events over the past few months and they have some absolute bangers lined up for the future. And of course, my boys and girls at NTFS (NoTimeForSpace) are making a huge name for themselves with their first-ever event (Break-Fast Club) being nothing but an absolute success. They have their second one lined up for us on the 19th of June and it’s sure to be even better than the first.

Music is something that has a special place in my heart, and Techno has the biggest room in the house.
